Karachi — City of Lights

Where the Arabian Sea meets a skyline that never sleeps. Karachi pulses with the energy of a city that is at once Pakistan’s financial engine, its cultural melting pot, and its gateway to the sea. From golden shorelines to grand colonial palaces, the city rewards every kind of traveller.

Three Must-Visit Landmarks

Karachi’s coastline, architecture, and maritime heritage offer three landmark experiences that capture the soul of this sprawling metropolis.

Coastline

Clifton Beach

Karachi’s iconic seafront promenade

Stretching along the city’s southern edge, Clifton Beach is the heartbeat of Karachi’s leisure culture. As the sun dips toward the Arabian Sea, families, couples, and street vendors converge on the shoreline in a carnival of colour and sound. Horse-drawn tongas trot along the sand, camel rides are a staple for first-timers, and food stalls dish out everything from spicy gola ganda (shaved ice) to freshly grilled seafood.

The promenade runs alongside Sea View, a wide coastal avenue lined with palm trees and snack carts. Sunset here is a spectacle — the sky turns deep orange over the water while kite-sellers hawk their wares and children chase balloons across the wet sand. For the full Karachi experience, visit on a Friday or Saturday evening when the beach is at its most alive.

Heritage Architecture

Mohatta Palace

A colonial gem of pink Jodhpur stone

Built in 1927 by Shivratan Chandraratan Mohatta, a wealthy Marwari businessman, Mohatta Palace is one of the finest examples of Indo-Saracenic architecture in South Asia. The building draws on Mughal, Rajput, and Sindhi design traditions, most visibly in its arched verandahs, ornate turrets, and the warm pink stone quarried in Jodhpur specifically for its construction.

After Partition, the palace served as the residence of Fatima Jinnah, sister of Pakistan’s founder. Today it operates as a museum and cultural centre, hosting rotating exhibitions of contemporary Pakistani art alongside permanent displays of decorative arts, furniture, and archival photographs that chart Karachi’s transformation from a small fishing village to a modern metropolis. The manicured gardens surrounding the building are peaceful enough to justify a visit on their own.

Maritime Heritage

Pakistan Maritime Museum

Where naval history meets open sky

Located in the Clifton neighbourhood near the Karachi Navy Dockyard, the Pakistan Maritime Museum is a sprawling open-air and indoor complex dedicated to the country’s naval and seafaring history. Its centrepiece is PNS Babur, a retired destroyer that visitors can board and explore deck by deck, from the torpedo bays to the officers’ quarters.

The museum grounds display a remarkable collection of retired vessels, aircraft, submarines, and heavy weaponry. Indoor galleries trace the history of the Pakistan Navy from its formation in 1947 through key engagements, using dioramas, archival maps, and original artefacts. A separate children’s section makes it one of the more family-friendly cultural stops in the city. Entry fees are minimal and the campus is large enough to spend a full morning.

Plan Your Visit

Karachi is a year-round destination, but the season and time of day can make a significant difference to your experience.

Best Time to Visit

November through February is the most comfortable window. Temperatures stay between 18 °C and 28 °C, humidity drops, and the sea breeze is pleasant. Avoid the monsoon months (July–August) if you want dry beach days.

Getting Around

Ride-hailing apps are the most convenient way to move between landmarks. Rickshaws work well for short distances. For Clifton and Defence, most attractions cluster within a 10–15 minute drive of each other.

Where to Eat

Do not leave Karachi without trying biryani at one of the Burns Road restaurants, nihari from a street stall in the old city, or fresh seafood at the Fish Harbour near Keamari. Street food is as much a part of the city as its landmarks.
