Hunza Valley — Roof of the World

Nestled among the towering peaks of the Karakoram range in Gilgit–Baltistan, Hunza Valley is one of the most spectacular destinations on Earth. Its dramatic landscapes of snow-capped mountains, crystal-clear rivers, and ancient terraced farms have drawn travellers, photographers, and mountaineers for generations. The valley’s warm, hospitable people and rich cultural heritage make every visit unforgettable.

Rakaposhi Viewpoint

Rakaposhi, standing at 7,788 metres, is the 27th highest mountain in the world and one of the most visually striking peaks visible from any road on the planet. The viewpoint along the Karakoram Highway, located near the village of Ghulmet, offers an unobstructed panorama of the mountain’s massive glaciated face rising almost vertically from the valley floor.

What makes this viewpoint extraordinary is the sheer proximity — few places in the world allow you to stand at just 2,000 metres elevation and gaze directly at a summit nearly 6,000 metres above you. On clear mornings, the peak glows pink and gold at sunrise, creating one of Pakistan’s most photographed moments.

Attabad Lake

Attabad Lake was created in January 2010 when a massive landslide at Attabad village dammed the Hunza River, submerging several villages and a section of the Karakoram Highway. What began as a natural disaster has since become one of the most stunningly beautiful lakes in northern Pakistan.

The lake stretches over 21 kilometres and is famous for its vivid turquoise colour, caused by glacial minerals suspended in the water. Visitors can take boat rides across the lake, enjoying dramatic views of the surrounding Karakoram mountains reflected in the still, jewel-toned surface. The rebuilt highway now includes tunnels carved through the mountains, making the journey itself an engineering marvel.

Baltit Fort

Baltit Fort is a 700-year-old fortification perched on a hilltop above the town of Karimabad, serving as the ancestral seat of the Mirs (rulers) of Hunza for centuries. The fort’s architecture is a fascinating blend of Tibetan and local Hunza styles, with intricately carved wooden balconies, thick stone walls, and a commanding position that offers sweeping views of the valley below.

Restored in the 1990s by the Aga Khan Trust for Culture, the fort is now a UNESCO World Heritage tentative site and houses a museum displaying traditional artefacts, royal garments, and ceremonial objects from Hunza’s princely past. A visit to Baltit Fort is a journey through the centuries-old history and culture of one of Pakistan’s most isolated and beautiful regions.
